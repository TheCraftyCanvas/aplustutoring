Sir Ken Robinson

Encouraging Creativity in Our Classrooms


Hailed as an "expert in creativity, innovation and human resources," Sir Ken Robinson has dedicated his life and research to bringing creativity back to the classroom. He calls it a "revolution in the culture of education." Robinson believes that many of the world's current educational institutions "stifle creativity," ultimately leading to economic disaster, according to an interview with CNN.

 

After becoming famous through multiple 18-minute TED talks, Robinson has participated in developing strategies for countries, companies, and schools to encourage creativity and innovation, and to boost economic development.  He also authored the New York Times bestseller The Element: How Finding Your Passion Changes Everything and Out of our Minds: Learning to be Creative.

 

Robinson insists that there are many great teachers worldwide who are ready to bring on this educational revolution. "The answer is not to standardize education, but to personalize and customize it to the needs of each child and community," he said in the interview. Robinson emphasizes that children should be pushed to think creatively and to innovate in the classroom, just as adults should be pushed to do this in the workplace.

 

Robinson points to mass standardized testing, and the tendency to "teach to the test," as some of the leading problems stifling creativity in the classroom. He also blames the increased reliance on prescription drugs such as Ritalin, "that keep students' minds from wandering to things they naturally find more interesting." Removing classes in arts, humanities, and physical education due to budget cuts has also worsened the problem, Robinson said.

 

"The waste of talent in education is not deliberate," he said to CNN. "Teachers are as anxious about this as everyone else, but many of them feel trapped in the awkward groping of national reform policies, many of which misunderstand the problems as well as the solutions."

 

When invited to speak at an innovation conference in Cannes, Robinson recounted his time leading the U.K. National Commission on Creativity, Education and the Economy. In an article in Fast Company, he mentioned that he remembers the Secretary of State telling him, "We're very committed to creativity in education but we've got to get literacy and numeracy right first." Robinson said it was a misunderstanding of how it all works. "It's like saying we're going to bake a cake and if it works out, then we'll put the eggs in," he said. "If you want people to be literate, you have to get them passionate about reading and that's a creative job. To think of it as an afterthought or in conflict of the core purposes, is a misconception of what creativity is."

 

Robinson also encourages educators not to leave feelings out of science. He believes we should work to bridge the gap between thinking and feeling, the "schism between arts and the sciences," he argued in a commentary in the Huffington Post. "The sciences are thought to be all about truth and objectivity; the arts about feelings and creativity," but that's not the case for either discipline, he wrote.

 

Inspiring creativity is allowing people to discover passions and seek out alternative ways of solving problems, Robinson suggests in his books. "The role of a creative leader is not to have all the ideas; it's to create a culture where everyone can have ideas and feel that they're valued."

 

Robinson's TED talks have been viewed online more than 12 million times. To learn more about TED talks, and some of the organization's other projects, including TED-Ed, read our blog post or visit TED's website.

Learning Ally - Helping Those With Disabilities Read

One out of five Americans has a learning disability, and nearly 14 million people in the U.S. have some type of vision problem which makes reading difficult, according to the National Center for Learning Disabilities and the National Eye Institute. These numbers have continued to grow over the years as more accurate diagnoses are made.

   

Learning Ally is a national nonprofit organization whose mission is to "Promote personal achievement when access and reading are barriers to learning, by advancing the use of accessible and effective educational solutions." In order to fulfill this mission, Learning Ally offers a collection of more than 75,000 digitally recorded narrations of textbooks and other literature. The recordings are stored in an online database, easily downloadable for those who need them. The organization provides audio recordings for more than 300,000 users with limited reading ability due to disability.

 

Founded in 1948 as Reading for the Blind, aiding soldiers with lost vision from combat, the organization's popularity has continued to grow over the last 64 years. The name was changed to Reading for the Blind and Dyslexic, as its base began to expand in 1995. In 2011, its members suggested another name change, to Learning Ally, because they didn't want to be "labeled or typecast with a specific disability. Members just wanted the same opportunities to succeed that others enjoy," according to their website.

 

Headquartered in Princeton, New Jersey, this 501(c)3 boasts more than 6,000 volunteers across the U.S. Volunteers do everything from reading textbooks for live recordings to processing the materials into the digital database.

 

Learning Ally hosts Record-A-Thons across the country, encouraging people to "read for a reason," NBC29 Charlottesville reported in March.

 

In order to be eligible for membership and access to the recordings, individuals must be diagnosed with a documented learning disability, visual impairment, blindness, low vision, dyslexia, a reading disorder, and/or a physical disability that prevents digesting printed text. Annual membership for an individual costs $99 and financial aid is available.

 

Referring to dyslexic students, Learning Ally's website states,  "Because these students must decode every word, one at a time, they often lose the gist of what they are reading. It's not that they can't understand the content; they just have a barrier in their way - the printed word," These students can benefit from "supported reading," or listening while reading.

 

Learning Ally's research demonstrates that students using audiobooks reach significant milestones in the classroom: 

 

  • 76% improved reading comprehension
  • 76% increased interest in reading
  • 52% improved reading accuracy
  • 61% increased self-confidence
  • 67% increased motivation

 

To get more information on how to provide Learning Ally materials in your school, visit the Schools and Educators section. To learn more about volunteer opportunities with Learning Ally, contact its King of Prussia, PA recording studio.
