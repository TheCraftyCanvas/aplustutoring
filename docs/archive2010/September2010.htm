Should Your Student Consider a Non-traditional College or Program?
It can be a challenging process for college-bound students to find a college that is the right "fit" because the options are so wonderfully varied. There are large public universities, small liberal arts colleges, urban schools, women's colleges, schools that specialize in the performing or decorative arts, and so on. Once you also factor in the wide array of geographical locations in the United States, it is no wonder the task of choosing a college can seem daunting. There is another sub-category of school to consider, though, and that is the non-traditional college.  Here is a list of some to consider:

Bard College at Simon's Rock, also known as the "early" college, is a unique school of just 400 students located in the heart of the Berkshire Mountains of Massachusetts. With a focus on liberal arts and sciences, it only accepts students after 10th or 11th grade in high school, and offers both A.A. and B.A. degrees. Simon's Rock provides an exceptional, intense experience for younger students who are seeking a different kind of educational path.

St. John's College is one college with two locations: one in Annapolis, MD, and the other in Santa Fe, NM.  Each campus strives to maintain an undergraduate population of fewer than 500 students who may transfer freely between the two campuses. St. John's has no electives, no majors, and no departments. Using a discussion-based class format, all students follow the same four-year curriculum based on the "Great Books" of the Western tradition--and approximately 80% go on to graduate school. This is a college for true intellectuals who wish to be challenged by a rigorous course of study in the classics.

Hampshire College's tag line is "Become the architect of your own education." Crafting one's own educational plan is at the heart of Hampshire's philosophy. With faculty advice and guidance, each student creates his own individualized program of study which is then followed through three levels known as the divisional system: Division I (basic studies), Division II (the concentration), and Division III (advanced study, which includes completing a major independent project). Emphasis is placed on interdisciplinary and collaborative studies, and only narrative evaluations are provided to students. There are no grades. As part of the five-college consortium that includes the University of Massachusetts at Amherst, Smith College, Mount Holyoke College, and Amherst College, Hampshire College offers its student body of approximately 1,400 a non-traditional education in a very traditional, charming New England setting.

Three-year programs: A small but growing trend in higher education is the completion of an undergraduate degree in three rather than four years. There are several reasons why this option is attractive. For parents, it can be a huge money-saver. By condensing your daughter's college career into three years, you save considerably on nine months of room and board, and even on books and tuition in cases when courses are pared down to just the essentials. For a very motivated student who wants to jump-start his or her career or who plans to go on to graduate school, shrinking the amount of time spent as an undergraduate makes sense.  Also, priority registration is often a perk of these three-year programs, so students get first dibs at classes that they otherwise might have been shut out of.

While some colleges formally offer accelerated courses of study, it is still possible to graduate early from schools that don't advertise this option. For example, the same end can be achieved if you take AP exams in high school and score well enough on them to obtain college credit, combined with taking a heavier-than-usual course load each semester and taking summer classes. The pace and intensity of such a workload are not for the faint of heart, though, and socializing, employment, and extracurricular activities often fall by the wayside. For the names of specific colleges that offer 3-year programs and a more in-depth discussion of this topic in the Wall Street Journal, follow this link.
 
Going Abroad: Many American students opt to study abroad for a semester or a year in college, but if you have an adventurous teenager who wants to experience life outside of the United States, why not consider a college in Canada, Scotland, or England for all four years? There are many distinguished institutions of higher learning that are worth exploring: McGill University in Quebec, Oxford and Cambridge in England, and the University of Edinburgh in Scotland, among others. Travel costs are obviously a consideration. The possibility of coming home for just a weekend diminishes with distance. And logistical issues such as obtaining a student visa and communicating long-distance (the cost of cell phone calls can mount) need to be sorted out. However the tuition and room and board at prestigious McGill University cost about a third less than that of a comparable private American university.  If you hail from Philadelphia and your son expresses interest in attending McGill, consider this--he would be even farther way if he went to the University of Michigan!
Rethinking Study Habits




A recent article in the New York Times sheds new light on old ways of thinking about what are considered "good" study habits. Such things as finding a quiet place to study, sticking to a homework routine, setting goals, and avoiding bribes may be a bunch of hogwash, according to new information.

In recent years, several techniques that run counter to the prevailing wisdom about how to "properly" study have been shown by researchers to improve learning in students of all ages.  Varying the room in which one studies appears to enhance retention, for example.  Alternating the kind of information studied at one sitting--not focusing solely on memorizing vocabulary, for instance--also appears to deepen one's grasp of the material. The concept of distinct learning styles (visual learning vs. auditory learning, e.g.) and teaching styles has also been called into question.

For more information, read the New York Times article.
