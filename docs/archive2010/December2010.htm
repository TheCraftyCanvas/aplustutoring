Top Ten Things Colleges Look for in High School Students


Although A+ focuses on improving students' standardized test scores and school grades through its wide variety of test prep programs and one-on-one subject tutoring, we recognize that there are other components of a high school student's background that are also considered in the college admissions process. 


With that in mind, we wanted to share with you a list of the top ten qualities and experiences colleges look for in applicants. This list was compiled by the Independent Educational Consultants Association, based on a 2010 survey of member consultants.

  1. A rigorous high school curriculum that challenges the student and may include AP (Advanced Placement) or IB (International Baccalaureate) classes.
  2. Grades that represent strong effort and an upward trend. However, slightly lower grades in a rigorous program are preferred to all As in less challenging coursework.
  3. Solid scores on the SAT and ACT that are consistent with the student's high school performance.
  4. Passionate involvement in a few activities, demonstrating leadership and initiative. Depth, not breadth, of experience is most important.
  5. Letters of recommendation from teachers and guidance counselor that give evidence of integrity, special skills, positive character traits, and an interest in learning.
  6. A well-written essay that provides insight into the student's unique personality, values, and goals. The application essay should be thoughtful and highly personal. It should demonstrate careful and well-constructed writing.
  7. Special talents or experiences that will contribute to an interesting and well-rounded student body.
  8. Demonstrated leadership in activities. Colleges want people who will arrive prepared and willing to take leadership of student activities and events.
  9. Demonstrated intellectual curiosity through reading, school, leisure pursuits, and more.
  10. Demonstrated enthusiasm to attend, often exhibited by campus visits and an interview.
Source: Independent Educational Consultants Association
Net-price Calculators: Determining College Costs


Help is finally on the way for families struggling to get a handle on the tuition rates and financial aid opportunities available at the various colleges to which their son or daughter is interested in applying. Starting in the fall of 2011, the federal government is requiring all U.S. colleges to include a tool called a net-price calculator on their websites. The goal of this new initiative is to make the financial aspect of attending college more transparent and to make it easier for families to compare the cost of attending one institution over another.


Variables such as household income, savings, mortgage, and number of other family members in college will be considered; the calculator will crunch those numbers and produce an estimated cost of attending the college. How merit and need-based financial aid will be factored into these calculations remains to be seen, especially when a student hasn't even applied yet. Many details still need to be worked out, but colleges have a lot hanging in the balance as this new federal requirement could have a powerful impact on admissions outcomes. CISV: Children's International Summer Village


If you are looking for something unique for your child to get involved in this summer, consider CISV (an acronym for Children's International Summer Village), a unique educational program that has been in existence since 1950. CISV offers a range of both domestic and international programs for young people aged 11 through 18, beginning with its flagship program called "Village." Village is a four-week international camp for 11-year olds that is held in various locations all over the globe. Village offers an exciting, mind-opening experience that emphasizes such values as global friendship, peace education, and multi-cultural awareness. Each Village has delegations from 12 countries, and each delegation consists of 2 girls, 2 boys, and an adult leader. The Philadelphia chapter of CISV is currently accepting delegate applications to its Summer 2011 Villages in Cincinnati, Ohio and Porvoo, Finland. They are also accepting leader applications for adults aged 21 and up. The application deadline is January 3, 2011. To find out more about CISV and its various programs and to download a Village or leader application, please visit philly.cisvusa.org and click on "2011 Program Opportunities."

Keystone Exams: A Primer


In just four years, beginning with students in the classes of 2015 and 2016, Pennsylvania will institute new high school graduation requirements that include the use of newly developed end-of-course assessments called the Keystone Exams. These exams are designed to assess proficiency in a wide range of subjects including Algebra I, Algebra II, Biology, Chemistry, Civics and Government, English Composition, Geometry, Literature, U.S. and World History. This new testing program is just one component of the state's high school graduation requirements. Students must also meet local district credit and attendance requirements and complete a culminating project, among other requirements.  Local districts can also opt for alternative graduation requirements that include independently validated local assessments or AP or IB exams, or some combination thereof.


Specifically, the classes of 2015 and 2016 will take the Keystone Exams in the following subjects: Algebra I, Biology, English Composition, and Literature. The Keystone Exams will serve as the final exams in each of these courses and will count for about one-third of the final course grade.  No student will be denied a high school diploma based on Keystone Exams alone, as about 2/3 of any course grade will remain teacher-graded.


One benefit of the Keystone Exams is that they will replace the PSSA (Pennsylvania System of School Assessment) that 11th graders must currently take and will reduce annual testing time by 18 hours.  In addition, if a student does not score at a level of "proficient" on a Keystone Exam after two attempts, he or she will be eligible to complete a project-based assessment as an alternate way of meeting this graduation requirement of proficiency.  For more information about the Keystone Exams, click this link.
