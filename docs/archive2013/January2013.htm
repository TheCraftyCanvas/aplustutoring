A New Twist on College Admissions Essays

 

Colleges are asking prospective students to show a little more flair this year, giving students room to creatively express their personalities in their application essays. New essay questions require students to branch out from the standard responses so often submitted with college applications.

 

Inspired by schools such as the University of Chicago that have long celebrated "provocative" essay questions, a number of colleges have decided to add supplemental writing portions that will allow admissions committees to become more acquainted with prospective students.

 

The following are a few examples of these new essay questions cited by Larry Gordon in "New college application questions encourage creative thinking," an article in the Los Angeles Times:

  • "Identify and describe a personal habit or idiosyncrasy -- of any nature -- that helps define you," Occidental College, Los Angeles, California
  • "Celebrate your nerdy side," Tufts University, Medford, Massachusetts
  • "Think of things that fascinated you when you were 10 years old -- what has endured?" Wake Forest University, Winston-Salem, North Carolina
  • "Please describe an unusual way in which you have fun," California Institute of Technology, Pasadena, California

A few other creative prompts from Kat Cohen's Huffington Post article, "The truth about college admissions essays" include:

  • "Explain Unicorns," University of Michigan's Honors Program, Ann Arbor, Michigan
  • "What is your favorite word and why?" University of Virginia, Charlottesville, Virginia
  • "A package arrives at your door. After seeing the contents you know it's going to be the best day of your life. What's inside and how do you spend your day?" Brandeis University, Waltham, Massachusetts

Schools are looking for students to answer the new questions in quirky and creative ways. Cohen said the essays serve as a way for admissions officers to determine, "Who are you? Will you make a valuable contribution to our campus community? What type of character traits do you possess? Are you responsible? Shy? Creative? A leader? A nonconformist? How have you shown your intellectual vitality?"  

 

The University of Dayton, a Catholic university in Dayton, Ohio, encouraged students to create videos to answer the essay question "What does it mean to be a servant leader?" and post to their Twitter and Facebook accounts. According to a Chicago Tribune article by Bonnie Miller Rubin, students were also invited to submit video essays to Tufts University and George Mason University.

 

Rubin also found a handful of schools asking students to give a "tweet-like" response, prompting students to answer in 25 words or less. Here are a few examples:

  • "My favorite thing about last Tuesday?" University of Maryland, College Park, Maryland
  • "What is your favorite ride at the amusement park? How does this reflect your approach to life?" Emory University, Atlanta, Georgia
  • "The best movie of all time," Columbia University, New York, New York

The trend started with an incredible range of responses to the University of Chicago's famous essay questions, Rubin said. Each year UC emails its current students asking for new essay questions. The winners from this year include:

  • "So where is Waldo, really?" Responses included one student leading the admissions officers on a scavenger hunt through the library, Gordon noted in his article. Others submitted responses using Waldo as a metaphor for international affairs.
  • "A man cannot be too careful in the choice of his enemies. -Oscar Wilde. Othello and Iago. Dorothy and the Wicked Witch. Autobots and Decepticons. History and art are full of heroes and their enemies. Tell us about the relationship between you and your arch-nemesis (either real or imagined)."

Though the questions may leave one student scratching his head while another can't wait to begin typing, one thing is certain: Colleges are trying to find ways to get to know students beyond grades and test scores.

Preparation for College Requires More Than Academics

When an 18-year-old finishes high school, packs up her belongings and leaves for college, she often has no idea what her life will be like over the next four years. Though her high school Advanced Placement (AP) classes may have prepared her for college courses, living on her own will be a major adjustment.

 

Another student in her dorm, a young man, may feel ready to take on the world, just as he did on his varsity teams in high school. However he is as yet unaware of the challenges to come, such as how making friends in college will be quite different from the way it was in grade school or high school. He is also unprepared for the level of responsibility required to balance schoolwork, friends and extracurricular activities in college.

 

The issues these two students will face in adjusting to college illustrate what some educators believe is lacking in our college preparatory curriculum: practical information about what to expect in college, and a path toward adjustment.

 

Education Week's Caralee J. Adams focuses on this issue in her article, "'Soft Skills' pushed as part of college readiness." The article notes that a number of education experts say that students' lack of "soft skills" such as time management, socialization skills, and dealing with failure, contribute to low college-completion rates in the United States.

 

"Among industrialized nations, the United States ranks ninth in the world in enrollment but last in completion rates, according to an analysis of 18 countries by the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development," Adams wrote.

 

In order to address the problem, a number of high schools have added soft skills education to curricula, and some teachers are working to prepare students ahead of time by not always allowing them to rely on help from parents. 

 

Emphasizing "responsibility and self-regulation" are key ways that parents and teachers can help students learn more about what college life entails, says Mandy Savitz-Romer, a researcher at the Harvard Graduate School of Education, and author of Ready, Willing, and Able: A Developmental Approach to College Access and Success. 

 

According to the article, some students have a difficult time adjusting to college life, in part because well-meaning parents are overly supportive while the students are in high school. Adams quoted Pam Mintz, a lecturer at a YMCA in Montgomery County, Maryland, "As much as humanly possible, never do for a child what they can do for themselves."  

 

Mintz recommends that parents ask children to prepare for life outside home by managing money, completing chores around the house, and learning how to cope with mistakes. 

 

Harlan Cohen, author of The Naked Roommate and 107 Other Issues You Might Run Into In College, was also quoted in the Education Week article. His book helps students prepare for the uncomfortable parts of college, such as "people crying, struggling, vomiting, dealing with roommate conflict or heartache." The book aims to give students the "communication skills to navigate through adversity that is part of the normal transition to college."
