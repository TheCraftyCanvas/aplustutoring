The Genius of Creativity

 

What do Albert Einstein, Benjamin Franklin and Steve Jobs all have in common? According to biographer Walter Isaacson, all three focused on the "intersection where creativity meets technology," or simply put, the art of science. It's in this intersection where real value is created, Isaacson said during a Town Hall Lecture Series at Ringling College in Sarasota, Florida on January 15, 2013.

 

Throughout the lecture, Isaacson reflected on the traits that made each leader stand out in history, the characteristics that made them creative.

 

Interestingly enough, many experts agree that the special creativity factor that these three inventors shared is not limited to those who are especially gifted. In an interview with Mashable's Josh Catone, Jonah Lehrer, author of Imagine: How Creativity Works, argued that creativity is something that can be practiced and learned.

 

There is a practical value to studying the traits possessed by each of these historical figures. The exercises below are recommended by some experts to stimulate creative thinking in the classroom.

 

Albert Einstein - Rebellious Curiosity

 

Einstein taught us that it pays off to "always be willing to think differently, and be slightly rebellious," Isaacson said. This kind of "rebellious curiosity" is what helped Einstein transform our knowledge of physics.

 

Behavior therapist and consultant Andrea Kuszewski recommends that teachers bring creative curiosity into the classroom by encouraging exploratory learning, rather than linear thinking which only looks for one answer to a problem. In her Scientific American guest blog, "The educational value of creative disobedience," Kuszewski said parents could also use this approach. "When your child asks you a question, rather than immediately delivering the answer, hold back for a moment, and say, 'I'm not sure--what do you think?'" she wrote.

 

Benjamin Franklin - Listening

 

Franklin demonstrated the significance of "listening to other people, forming groups, and trying to find common ground based on enduring values," Isaacson said.

 

In a study published in the journal Science, Emory medical professor Robert DeHaan insists that science classes that leave creativity out of the discussion are doing students a disservice. "Students need to be repeatedly reminded and shown how to be creative, to integrate material across subject areas, to question their own assumptions, and to imagine other viewpoints and possibilities," DeHaan noted.

 

Brainstorming sessions and collaboration to come up with a solution can help cultivate this creativity, DeHaan said. Students should be asked to generate a number of ideas about possible solutions before coming to a conclusion.

 

Steve Jobs - Brutal Honesty and Attention to Detail

 

Steve Jobs, Apple's former CEO, will forever be remembered for his brutal honesty and attention to detail. Isaacson recounted a story that Jobs told him while he was working on his biography. When Jobs was 6 years old, he was asked by his dad to paint the family's fence. There was one section of the fence that backed up to something else and wasn't visible, but Job's dad still wanted him to paint it. When the young Jobs complained that no one would ever see it, his dad replied, "but you'll know."

 

Later in life, Jobs would use this same quote while working with his engineers and programmers as they built the inside of Apple computers, Isaacson said. Jobs was a firm believer that a real artist cares about the parts unseen, and often that meant his coworkers were scrambling at the last minute to make things perfect.

 

Steve Jobs was not alone in his belief in brutal honesty to foster creative thinking. Jonah Lehrer agreed that introducing "debate and dissent" into brainstorming sessions helps people weed out the bad ideas and push for something even better. "It is the human friction that makes the sparks," he said in the Mashable interview. "In fact, some studies suggest that encouraging debate and dissent can lead to a 40% increase in useful new ideas from the group."

 

Opening the Mind to Creative Thought

 

All three of these inventors understood that creative thinking would allow them to transform traditional methods of solving a problem. They seemed to "sense that they were part of something larger than themselves; they felt they were part of history," Isaacson said in the lecture. But, he cautioned the audience, "biographies are not how-to manuals, they just give an account of a person and his or her flaws." You can aspire to be like someone from history, but to be truly creative you must forge your own path.

Featured ACT Student: Dan Graeff
Dan Graeff

Daniel Graeff, a highly motivated and focused junior at Wissahickon High School, wanted his college board test scores to accurately reflect the determination and hard work he dedicated to preparing for college.  

 

Daniel and his family followed a recommendation from his college advisor to prepare for his college admissions tests with A+ Test Prep and Tutoring.  

 

He began the program in July by taking two full-length diagnostic tests, the ACT and the SAT, in order to determine which test better reflected his skill set. Based on the results of the diagnostic exams, he decided he would prepare for the ACT.

 

Over four months, Daniel completed thirteen 1.5-hour A+ tutoring sessions at his home. The sessions alternated between science and math preparation with Maggie Feinberg, and reading and English work with Christine Sweger.

 

"One thing really stood out," Christine Sweger said, "He was really committed to doing practice tests. He was very interested in finding his weaknesses and strengths. He was interested in learning."

 

And, according to Maggie, "He was very engaged, very prepared and always completed everything I asked him to do. He went above and beyond, he even asked for extra problems and extra work."

 

Daniel was impressed that Maggie, an analyst at Merck and Co., tutored as a side job to share some of the knowledge she used every day at work. "She was good at letting me do it on my own," Daniel said. "If I needed help, she would critique it and give me the tips I needed to be successful."

 

Maggie showed Daniel that he did not necessarily have to know the material in the science section to be able to solve the problems. Understanding the thinking and reasoning behind the science problems is the important thing. "Science on the ACT is difficult; the problems can be very intimidating," she said. "Some of the problems involve studies I learned in college." The trick, according to Maggie, was to show Daniel that he could succeed on this kind of problem.

 

Christine was also a huge help, according to Daniel. She taught him strategies and techniques for answering reading comprehension questions, such as underlining topic sentences, he said. Christine also taught him "how to communicate in an effective way that really hits home," he said, which helped him prepare for the essay questions.

 

When Daniel sat down to take the ACT in December, he felt nervous. "It was nerve-racking, knowing how this could have an impact on which college I got in to," he admitted. He didn't let that distract him, however.

 

"I kept focus as my top priority," he said. "I knew I had the knowledge to back it up. It was big and really helpful in my mindset."

 

When he finished, Daniel felt not only relieved, but also confident. Typically English is not his strong suit, but after setting his pencil down after the test, Daniel felt his essay "was the icing on the cake." All of his motivation and determination paid off. Daniel's composite score on the ACT was a 31, ranking him in the 97th percentile on the exam.

 

Daniel said the skills he practiced with his A+ tutors have also helped him in his classes. "When you face something similar, like in history courses, when you are asked to read chapters in a book, you are able to comprehend them better," he said.

 

Daniel is just beginning his college search, with plans to visit a number of Pennsylvania-area schools this spring to learn more about engineering and business programs. The 16-year-old keeps himself busy with track and cross-country practice throughout the year, along with a number of activities at his church, including assisting in the ministry.

 

"I really enjoyed working with Daniel," Maggie said, "He's so motivated, it makes it worthwhile tutoring."
