Are You a Soprano? A Tenor? Finding Your Voice for the College Essay
 

 

When plenty of professional authors find it difficult to express themselves in the first-person singular, is it fair to expect 17-year-olds to turn out perfectly tuned personal writing? College admissions officers believe that the one element of the application where a student's personality can emerge is the essay. But what if a high school junior or senior comes across as trite or vain or shallow simply because she is unfamiliar with this genre of writing?

 

The SAT essay is the type of essay that is typically taught in high schools, especially in courses such as A.P. English, which, according to the College Board that guides their curriculum, emphasize "expository, analytical and argumentative essays." Thus, when it is time to write the college application essay, which involves self-reflection, it is understandably difficult for many students to successfully write a type of prose with which they have had little or no experience.

 

College admissions officers, however, have made the personal essay a key part of the application. After all, they need something to separate the straight-A, community-serving, high-SAT-scoring, super-achieving musical athletes! According to a 2009 survey by the National Association for College Admission Counseling, 26 percent of admissions offices deemed the essay to be of "considerable importance" in deciding who is admitted. By contrast, in 1993, only 14 percent gave the essay such priority.

 

When approaching the essay, it is essential to find your own voice. Many writers worry that they haven't had enough life experience or exposure to great literature; it is important to remember, however, that smaller everyday subjects are more likely to produce a revealing picture of yourself. Another tip is to throw fear out the window: dismiss thoughts about how naïve or silly you think the idea sounds. The goal is to be honest and forthcoming, but within boundaries. Dani Shapiro, the author of Devotion: A Memoir succinctly states this concept: "This is not your diary. You are not letting it all hang out."

 

And a few more words of advice from authors on first-person writing from authors:

 

Darin Strauss, author of Half a Life offers the following 'trick' to help students get over their block about writing in the first person: Begin by writing your essay in the third person. Once you have completed the essay switch back from third to first person.

 

According to Ander Monson, author of Vanishing Point: Not a Memoir, "We find ourselves not by turning inward toward what we imagine is inside us, but by the act of looking outward at the world....In other words, the best way to write about ourselves is to write about something specific in the world. We don't write about ourselves. We write ourselves."

 

Math, Science, and Business-Oriented Websites for Kids, Parents, and Teachers
 

 

 

We have compiled a short list of excellent (and fun!) websites for students, parents, and teachers who would like extra information or who are looking for fun ways to learn about math, science, and business.

 

Elementary and Middle School Level

Math Playground has interactive games to make learning math fun. Students can practice math skills with logic games, word puzzles, or virtual manipulatives (tools to help visualize math concepts).

 

Funbrain.com is another site with interactive math games such as Soccer Shootout (have fun with fractions and win the soccer game) and Shape Surveyor (train to be an archaeologist). This site also has Words, Universe and Culture sections, and isn't just for kids - there are also useful resources for teachers and parents.

Middle and High School Level

Knowledge@Wharton High School (KWHS) is the latest addition to the knowledge network at Wharton. It is an online journal for high school students interested in finding out more about the world of business. The site offers articles, interviews, a video glossary of business terms, an interactive stock market game, and many other features, all of which are designed to teach high school students more about business and finance. The free site, which is in Spanish as well as English, also includes a portal where teachers can share lesson plans, blogs, and other resources relevant to their business curricula.

 

S.O.S. Mathematics has more than 2,500 math pages filled with easy-to-understand explanations, from Algebra to Differential Equations to Calculus. High school students, teachers, or any adult learner can get help with homework, get a memory refresher (useful for when your child asks you for help with his homework!), or prepare for a test. 

All Levels

National Library of Virtual Manipulatives (NLVM) is an NSF-supported project that began in 1999 to develop a library of uniquely interactive, web-based virtual manipulatives for mathematics instruction (K-12 emphasis). The NLVM is a resource from which teachers, tutors, and parents may freely draw to enrich their mathematics instruction. This site, in both English and Spanish, is very user-friendly.

 

Franklin Institute "Resources for Science Learning" has science headlines, resources for learners and educators, activities, articles, workshops, and more. There is an audio link to NPR's "Science Friday" where the curious can listen to weekly studies and discoveries on topics ranging from cats' whiskers to outer space. This site is bound to capture the interest of children and adults alike. Moreover, it highlights exhibits, events, and lectures that will get the whole family together for a day of science.
Featured Student
Tim Frank

This month we are pleased to profile Tim Frank, a junior at Christopher Dock Mennonite High School in Lansdale, PA. Tim came to A+ for assistance because his SATscores, in the verbal section in particular, were not as high as he wanted them to be.  According to Tim, A+ provided a very comfortable, non-stressful environment in which to study. With personalized help, it was possible for him to focus on the areas in which he needed the most assistance and to get advice on how to approach the test in general. Tim took the SAT again in January 2011, and saw a 200-point overall improvement. Good work, Tim!

 

Emily Novitski, Tim's verbal tutor, remarked: "He had a positive attitude and applied what he learned during the sessions. Although Tim did not achieve his score goal on some of the practice tests, he earned an excellent score on the SAT." Matt McAuliffe, who worked with Tim on math, said: "The first thing that struck me about Tim was his enthusiasm to work on SAT skills even though we met on Saturday afternoon.  Students who are excited to work on Saturdays are rare!  In addition, Tim was particularly receptive to applying new strategies to the test.  He was eager to improve and was willing to use the new strategies to achieve this goal."

 

In addition to being a sports fan and an athlete on his school's varsity teams, Tim is a member of the National Honor Society and the Junior Class Vice-President. Although he is still in the process of finding colleges to which he wants to apply, Tim is already thinking about studying to be a physical therapist.  

Featured Website

Play a vocabulary game and feed the hungry!!

 

Freerice.com challenges you, the player, to answer increasingly difficult vocabulary questions (levels 1-60). For each correct answer, 10 grains of rice are donated through the UN World Food Programme to help end world hunger. It is great practice for the SAT verbal section or for any student who wants to work on vocabulary.  What a fantastic way to improve your writing and speaking and help others at the same time!

 

In addition to vocabulary, Freerice.com is continually adding questions in new subject areas. Those recently added include geography, chemistry, art, language, and math questions.
