Helping Kids Manage Stress
Exercise is one proven way to reduce stress.

College applications, bullying at school, a tough test, or a bad soccer game -- we've all seen the many ways our kids encounter stressful situations. Over the past several years, research has discovered that extreme external stress, such as physical and verbal abuse in the home, has the potential to cause long term damage to cognitive function. More recent research now demonstrates that children who feel anxious about daily situations can also experience stress's harmful effects on the brain.

A child's brain is in constant development, comparable to a piece of clay that is repeatedly stretched and reshaped. Stress is a normal part of the learning cycle and can even help the brain grow. Chronic stress, on the other hand, can cause damage.

 According to an article by Hank Pellsier of the non-profit, GreatSchools, stress hormones such as cortisol can build up in the brain and prevent growth. Joann Deak, an educator and psychologist, calls parents and teachers the "neurosculptors" of children. Managing stress levels, including anxiety about getting into college and getting good grades, is part of a parent's job. "Stress isn't bad," she said at a conference in Los Angeles, "but pervasive stress literally starts killing neurons."

 So what can you do?

Start monitoring your child's stress level. Some kids struggle more with making mistakes. Help your child understand that making mistakes is part of the learning process and actually helps your cognitive development.

 According to Pellsier's research, most doctors advise the cure-all mandate: exercise often. Exercise provides a variety of benefits, including having a relaxing effect, both physically and mentally. So encouraging your child to play outside or go to the gym is one of the best ways to reduce his stress. 

 

You also can teach your child breathing techniques which can help her remain calm when the pressure in school becomes excessive. Here is one technique:

  • Slowly take in a deep breath through your nose until your lungs are full and then let the air out through your mouth.  
  • With each breath, imagine your belly filling with air.  
  • Each time you blow out, the negativity is pushed out of your system. 

Work with your child to minimize the cause of stress. Help him develop plans to complete homework on time and study for the next test. Offer to spend time with her discussing tough questions or leafing through college brochures. Talk to your child's teachers about the best ways to prevent bullying from happening. 

 

Check with the teacher to see how your child seems to be coping with stress in school. If your child gets frustrated during specific lessons, consider spending extra time before or after class to work on that subject.

However, Pellsier's top stress-reducing tip was directed to parents: "Listen, don't lecture." Let your child know that you understand how she feels. Let her vent if she needs to, and tell her you will try to work it out together.  

Harvard's Dr. Roland Fryer on Improving American Education
Only 32 percent of fourth graders in the United States are proficient in reading. Just 39 percent are proficient in math, according to statistics from the National Assessment of Educational Progress.

The United States spends over $10,000 annually per student on primary and secondary education; more than almost any other country. But on international assessments which compare performance across the world, 15-year-old Americans rank 25th in math, 17th in science, and 12th in reading.

These statistics keep economist and Harvard professor Roland Fryer up at night. Fryer, the youngest African American to ever receive tenure from Harvard, has devoted his life to improving American education.

Fryer founded EdLabs at Harvard in 2008. EdLabs are a series of multi-million dollar experiments to discover how to bridge the achievement gap in the United States.

The 34-year-old professor is not without his critics. In 2011, he tested a $75 million incentives program, sparked by Fryer's background in economics. EdLabs paid students at 130 different schools in Dallas $2 to read a book, offered kids up to $50 for higher test scores in New York, and bribed children in Washington, DC to come to school without beating each other up. That experiment didn't work. Why? Fryer says despite having a reward waiting at the finish line, the kids didn't have the tools to accomplish the task at hand.

So what needs to change? Fryer boils it down to five main areas:

1. Better teachers, better principals

Fryer believes schools need to find leaders who inspire and are trained to take on the challenge. And teachers with a drive and a passion to help students need the support of formal training and continuous professional development to help them improve their students' reading and math skills. Teachers should achieve performance bonuses and merit pay, and should be constantly evaluated.

 2. Increased tutoring or one-on-one sessions

One of the most effective ways to reform the educational system is by giving students personalized attention during tutoring sessions, Fryer said. Devoting more time to one-on-one education increases children's ability to concentrate on the areas that they are struggling with and get feedback on their work. Tutoring should be structured and focused, with the first few minutes for personal conversation, giving the kid a chance to bond with a mentor they can look up to. The rest of the time should be devoted in timed segments to certain subjects.  

3. Higher expectations

Fryer advises teachers and administrators to: tell students what is expected of them; have high expectations for attendance rates; demand a 100 percent graduation and college application rate, etc. In addition, Fryer advises schools to send home a school-parent contract. This contract is not enforced, but is used as a tool to explain to parents what the school's expectations are and how they will be achieved. It asks parents to provide a quiet place where students can study at home.  

4. Longer school days, longer school year

Extending the time in school means increased time for students to learn. The longer day, longer year directly translates into higher performance.

5. Frequent evaluation

Increased testing and quizzing allow teachers to effectively assess where students are picking up the material and what they are having difficulty understanding. Fryer believes that data should be used to drive instruction. If the students do not comprehend the material, re-teach and re-test.

According to EdLabs' studies, these five areas are critical components of our nation's ongoing efforts to improve public education. There are schools that are doing amazing things and making big strides in bridging the achievement gap, Fryer said. Now we need to find ways to share the knowledge.

Google Science Fair 2012

 

The Internet's most popular search engine is looking for bright, young scientists to share their experiments with the world.

 

Google is hosting an online science fair for 13- to 18-year-old students with a drive to create, invent, discover and report on real-world investigations. Participants can enter the contest solo or in a group of up to three students.

 

Prizes include college scholarships, a trip to the Galapagos Islands with National Geographic Explorer, and internships with Google and other participating companies.

 

"Kids are born scientists," said Mariette DiChristina, editor-in-chief of Scientific American, a magazine co-hosting the interactive event. "They ask great questions and we should foster their efforts to learn the answers firsthand."

 

This is the second year that Google will host the largest online science fair. Last year the competition had over 10,000 contestants from more than 91 countries. This year submissions will be accepted in 13 different languages.

 

Daring students to jump in on the challenge, Google encourages students to start asking questions, explore the unknown, and begin digging up the answers.

 

Students are asked to "pose a question, develop a hypothesis and conduct science experiments to test it," according to the fair's website. What's new about the Google Science Fair is that the whole process will be outlined and submitted online.

 

Rather than tracing or tacking bubble letters to poster boards, participants put together a personalized Google site with the details of their project. Presentations and videos supplement the project, bringing the audience through each step of the procedure. The interactivity allows judges and contestants from around the world to feel as if they are in the same room witnessing the experiment.

 

But winning the fair is not easy. Last year's winner, Shree Bose, a 17-year-old from Ft. Worth, Texas, identified a way to improve the effectiveness of ovarian cancer chemotherapy drugs. After winning the grand prize, Bose had the opportunity to meet President Obama and was named one of Glamour Magazine's 21 Amazing Women of the Year.

 

Those who made it to the final round asked questions like, "What's the effect different marinades have on harmful carcinogens in grilled chicken?" and, "Can I teach a robot to learn English?" Other projects were designed to answer such questions as, "Can I build a faster sailboat?" and, "How does air quality affect reliance on asthma medications?"

 

This year projects are due April 1, with the first round of 90 finalists announced at the end of May. In early June, 15 global finalists will be flown to Google's headquarters in Mountain View, California for a final judging round on July 23.

 

Scientific American will also be selecting a winner whose project positively impacts a community or group through addressing a social, environmental, or health issue. This science fair star will walk home with $50,000 and a yearlong mentorship to accomplish his or her project's goals.

 

To learn more about Google's Science Fair, visit  www.google.com/events/sciencefair.
