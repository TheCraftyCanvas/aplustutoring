Featured Student: Katelyn Kline

 

 

High School: Hatboro-Horsham

SAT Score Improvement: 210 Points

 

Last January, Katelyn Kline sat down at a piano she hadn't touched since childhood. She started tinkering with different notes, teaching herself how to play a few songs. However, she found she wanted to learn how to play the music, not just to memorize songs. She decided to start piano lessons as a junior in high school. When Katelyn wants to learn something, she completely devotes herself to achieving her goals and to truly understanding what she is learning.

 

Knowing her daughter, Katelyn's mother recommended that she receive one-to-one instruction for the SAT. After studying on her own and completing a practice run with a test prep book, Katelyn was "ready for anything" that would boost her scores.

 

For ten weeks before her test, every Saturday afternoon from 11:30 AM to 1:00 PM, Katelyn worked with A+ Tutor Andrew Cies.  Andrew taught Katelyn pointers that she could use to her advantage during the test. Katelyn became comfortable learning which questions she could guess on and which she should skip, giving her the highest score possible.

 

For the math section, Andrew illustrated different ways to "guesstimate." On the reading portion, he suggested Katelyn underline the important parts of the passage, and write one or two words to sum up each paragraph. Andrew helped Katelyn "think of a bank of topics for the essay part, which could then be morphed to fit different questions," she said.

 

During her condensed tutoring schedule, Katelyn took the SAT three times: one at the very start of her tutoring, one halfway and one at the end.

 

Walking in to her final SAT on March 3rd, Katelyn was nervous. "I knew it was my last chance to take it," she said. Katelyn drew on all of the information that she and Andrew had covered.

 

She was relieved to see that the essay could easily be formulated from the bank of topics that she and Andrew had drawn up as practice. She left the test feeling much more confident.

 

When her scores came in, Katelyn was at school. She was so anxious to see if her hard work paid off that she had her mom text the results to her cell phone. Her score improved 210 points, ranking her in the 93rd percentile, according to the College Board.

 

Katelyn was ecstatic that she achieved her score goal on the SAT. Paired with her high GPA and a competitive class rank, she is well on her way to a strong application for competitive universities. She's currently touring the campuses of Penn State, University of North Carolina- Chapel Hill, University of Virginia and University of Maryland. Katelyn aims to major in engineering or pharmaceutical sciences.

 

Katelyn's high scores are "a testament to how much effort and time she put into the program," Andrew said. "Katelyn had an end goal in mind and she went on pursuing that goal, really targeting" until she succeeded, Andrew said.

 

In addition to her newfound hobby of playing the piano, Katelyn dances, competes on Hatboro-Horsham's varsity tennis team, and designs the school's literary magazine.

 

College Costs: On the Rise, Evolving and Negotiable

  

Tuition on the Rise

 

With government aid to public higher education institutions waning and tuition on the rise, college students are facing increasing financial pressure.

 

"Since 2001, tuition and fees at four-year public colleges in the United States have risen at an annual average of 5.6 percent," Andrew Leonard reported in a May 11 article in Salon. The cost of attending a four-year university is rising much faster than inflation.

 

The debate over whether to increase federal funding for higher education is a perennially hot topic in Washington. Opponents of increasing federal aid for higher education believe it only "fuels higher costs," and does not provide an incentive for institutions to look for ways to reduce spending.

 

Leonard argued against this idea. "For the large majority of college students, rising tuitions have nothing to do with the availability of student loans or Pell Grants. What's happening, instead, is that the burden of paying for college that was previously provided directly by government has now been shifted onto the backs of students, in the form of crippling debt."

 

"Elite private institutions that only accept a fraction of students can continue to raise tuition because students will still apply no matter the price tag," Leonard said. "For-profit schools, which rely heavily on government money, also need to be evaluated separately from 4-year state schools."

   

Charging More for Certain Majors

 

At some universities, it is now more expensive to major in math, science, engineering or business. More than 140 public schools have adopted this method, called differential tuition, according to a story by Alicia McCarty in USA Today. The increased tuition is an effort to raise funds while states cut back on higher education spending.

 

Critics of differential tuition are concerned that lower-income students will shy away from certain majors due to their higher price tag.

 

Negotiating College Costs

 

Interestingly, some schools are willing to negotiate the tuition rate.

 

Finance columnist Gail Marks-Jarvis of the Chicago Tribune wrote a story last month on how to bargain for a better deal on higher education. What most people don't know, she said, is that colleges have a little wiggle room when they send out financial aid packages.

 

The first thing Marks-Jarvis recommends is to "understand how much aid is likely." A family should estimate this by using an "expected family contribution" calculator, such as finaid!, or this listing from US News and World Report.

 

Marks-Jarvis insists that if a student is admitted to two comparable schools with different aid packages, "she should call the director of financial aid at the institution she would like to attend. She should explain that she wants to attend that institution, but that money is a serious concern." The student should be prepared to explain her family's circumstances, and let the director know what talents she would bring to the incoming class.

 

"Do homework to anticipate the financial aid director's response," Marks-Jarvis advised. If the student has SAT scores that are higher than the college's average range of scores, he should let the director know. If he can differentiate himself and demonstrate how he will add diversity to the pool of applicants, this is the time to boast. Research how much the average student pays in yearly tuition and work with the director to see if he is willing to offer additional aid.

Loans and Financial Aid: Potential Changes with the 2012 Presidential Election

As prices for higher education continue to rise, most students pursuing college degrees in the U.S. are forced to borrow from the federal government, private lenders or relatives. "94 percent of students who earn a bachelor's degree borrow to pay for higher education - up from 45 percent in 1993," according a study by the New York Times. This contributes to more than $1 trillion in outstanding student loans in the U.S., the study demonstrated.

 

"Average debt for bachelor degree graduates who took out loans ranges from under $10,000 at elite schools like Princeton and Williams College, which have plenty of wealthy students and enormous endowments, to nearly $50,000 at some private colleges with less affluent students and less financial aid," the study found.

 

As far as earnings go, it's a general rule of thumb that some college is better than no college, and a higher degree usually translates in higher pay. According to a study from Georgetown University's Center on Education and the Workforce, "accountants and auditors with a high school diploma make $1.5 million over a lifetime, compared with $1.7 million for accountants and auditors with some college, $2.4 million for accountants and auditors with a Bachelor's degree, and $3 million for accountants and auditors with a graduate degree. The same pattern generally holds within all occupations," The College Payoff study stated.

 

Although a college degree is still seen as a good investment, more and more people are defaulting on their student loans due to the tough job market.

 

In this article we will look at how the policies of the Obama Administration compare to those of a possible Romney Administration when it comes to financial aid for college.

 

The Obama administration is likely to continue on the path it set out on last fall. This plan includes capping student loan payments, debt repayment assistance, and consolidation of federal loans.

 

Obama's "Pay As You Earn" legislation reduced student loan repayments for new borrowers to a maximum of 15 percent of discretionary income immediately, and to 10 percent in 2014. In October 2011, Obama asked for the benefits to go into effect in 2012, "reducing monthly payments for more than 1.6 million student borrowers," according to a White House press release.

 

In addition, the administration has worked to disseminate additional 

educational information on student loans. The Department of Education has created an online tool tool for repaying loans and wants to create adatabase of university financial aid disclosure forms.

 

Loan consolidation has been another focus of the Obama administration. "Simplifying payment reduces the risk of default," according to a blog post by "Student Loan Ranger" Isaac Bowers of Equal Justice Works.

 

Finally, the Obama administration has pushed for regulations that require "career college programs to better prepare students for 'gainful employment' or risk losing access to Federal student aid," according to a release from the Department of Education. Operating on a premise of "three strikes you're out," poor performing colleges will lose access to federal student aid programs if less than 35 percent of students are able to repay loans, or if annual loan repayment is more than 30 percent of discretionary income or more than 12 percent of total earnings.

 

If Mitt Romney wins the election, students will see many of Obama's current higher education initiatives rescinded. At the end of May, Romney released his education plan named A Chance for Every Child.

 

According to Romney's plan, "America is fast becoming a society where education is unaffordable, a government loan is an entitlement, default is the norm, and loan forgiveness is the expectation." He believes that the government needs to step away from the student loan business, and leave that up to the private sector. The Obama administration calculated its loan consolidation project would save $68 billion over 11 years, but Romney's calculations say it will be "revenue-neutral," according to a Wall Street Journal article by Sara Murray and Stephanie Banchero.

 

Romney favors loans from private vendors, and less federal grants to colleges and universities. Increasing federal funding to higher institutions "only serves to drive tuition higher," his plan noted.

 

Romney argues that prioritizing access to higher education has pulled attention away from college completion rates, something he sees as a huge problem that needs to be addressed.

 

According to Romney's plan, the government should eliminate unnecessary data collection, and focus on collecting a few essential statistics from institutions including completion rates, loan repayment rates and future earnings of graduates.

 

Romney's plan acknowledges that rising tuition prices are a growing problem, but insists that allowing consumers to make their own choices and granting the higher education industry the ability to compete is the only way to drive down college costs.
