Admissions Officers' Tips for Applying to College 

Applying to college can sometimes feel like a game of chance. There is no way to know for sure who will win the game and be admitted to the school of hirs or her choice. Students going through the process repeatedly hear that there is no one formula for success.

 

However, as in any game, there are certain moves you can make and others you can avoid. In the U.S. News and World Report article, "What Not to Do When Applying to College: Admissions Officers Share Their Pet Peeves," Keith Sinzinger and Rett Fisher compiled a list of the things that irk college admissions officers the most.

 

Here are a number of their suggestions:

  • Don't pretend to be someone that you're not. It is better to give examples of a couple of activities that you are really passionate about, rather than a laundry list of extra curricular activities.
  • Double-check that you're submitting the right essay to the right school. There's nothing more embarrassing than accidentally telling a college that your dream school is somewhere else.
  • Don't send emails to admissions officers for the sake of making contact. It's not impressive to call schools with simple questions that could easily be answered by looking at the university's website or marketing materials.
  • For your recommendation letters, take time to find a teacher who can prove how hard you are willing to work. Don't simply as a teacher whose class you did well in.
  • Be smart on social media. Admissions officers are Internet savvy -- they can find you (and your pictures!) on Facebook, YouTube, Twitter, etc.
  • Always be polite and courteous to all admissions staff members and school employees.
  • Never miss a deadline. All colleges have hard application due dates. Many will not consider applications submitted after their deadline, even if just by a few minutes.
  • Pay attention to all directions on the application. If you don't include your resume in the appropriate box, some admissions officers will not dig through your application to find it.

 

In addition to the tips from the U.S. News and World Report article, the Daily Beast's "Top 10 College Admissions Tips" includes a few more reminders from admissions counselors:

  • Always apply early. Getting your application in early expresses your level of interest in the school, said Rick Clark, Georgia Tech's Director of Admissions. Admissions officers can tell when students put things off until the last minute, making applications and essays look rushed.
  • Visit the campus. Schools follow which applicants have made a visit and decided that the school could be a good fit.
  • "The 'perfect' essay is the wrong essay," advised Douglas Christiansen, vice provost for enrollment and dean of admissions at Vanderbilt University. "Do not pick a topic that you think we want to read about. The purpose of the essay is to help the admissions committee fully understand the potential difference you can make in the class and how your background and experience will move the campus community forward."

 

Another very helpful resource for students and parents is John Carpenter's book, Going Geek: What Every Smart Kid (and Every Smart Parent) Should Know About College Admissions , available through Amazon.com.

Tracking Grades and Attendance Online

 

In an ever-evolving digital age, many parents now have students' grades and attendance at their fingertips via their schools' websites. Some schools have even implemented technology which sends text alerts to parents when a student receives a poor grade, doesn't turn in an assignment, or is tardy to a class.

 

More than a quarter of U.S. public schools are now offering online grade and attendance tracking systems for parents, according to a Wall Street Journal article by Sue Shellenbarger, "When Curious Parents See Math Grades in Real Time."

 

According to the article, some parents are thrilled by the technology, as it allows them to keep up on homework assignments and studying. John Patriarche, a Chandler, Arizona father interviewed for the article said online grades help him discover his daughter's "weaknesses and improve her study and planning skills before it's too late to avert a bad grade."

 

The article cites a poll by parenting website SheKnows that found that out of 115 parents polled, "32% said online reports help them prod their children to study and get assignments in on time. But 49% said teachers don't keep their pages updated, 14% said grade and assignment information is inaccurate and 15% said their children resent such monitoring efforts."

 

The services are being offered by a number of private companies: Blackboard Engage, Pearson PowerSchool, and Skyward, among others.

 

Some teachers enjoy the efficiency and ease of modifying grades in the online systems, noted John Smith in a Yahoo blog post, "Online Grade Books: Pros and Cons for Students, Parents and Teachers."

 

However the technology can feel like an added burden to some teachers, who find themselves having to deal with parents who monitor their students' grades frequently. One Philadelphia principal interviewed for the article, Chris Lehmann, warned "too much emphasis on posting and viewing online grades risks making education 'a faster and faster hamster wheel.'" The founder of Science Leadership Academy, Lehmann told Shellenbarger that he gives his teachers ten days to enter grades because he wants to give them ample time to get the work done.

 

Sometimes the quick "real-time" access opens up possibilities for error. Missing a number when typing in a grade can make the difference between an A and an F, one frustrated mom featured in the article pointed out.

 

A handful of the current systems in place also have room for technology improvement, according to a few parents interviewed in the article. One mother mentioned that the automated text messages generated from her school district didn't distinguish which one of her kids it was referring to. A different parent complained that she needed three different sets of login information for the same system, one for each of her three children.
