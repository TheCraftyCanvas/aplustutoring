A Comparison of Local College Graduation Rates
 
Click on the graphic above to zoom in

 

In 2004, more than 4.3 million students enrolled full-time in U.S. colleges. A study released by The Chronicle of Higher Education followed these enrollees to document how many graduated from the same institution four years later, how many earned a degree six years later, and how many students dropped out.

 

According to the study, Pennsylvania's public colleges graduated nearly 40 percent of their full-time Bachelor's degree students in four years, and 62.1 percent in six years. PA ranks 11th among the 50 states and the District of Columbia in graduation rates for public colleges, the data show.

 

It is important to note that a portion of the students who did not graduate in four or six years transferred schools and were not accounted for in the data set. According to the National Student Clearinghouse Research Center, nearly one-third of students transfer schools during college. 

Harvard's Lawrence Summers Suggests Ways to Modernize and Improve Higher Education

With the expansion of technology and the ability to access knowledge on almost any subject at the tips of our fingers, university administrators and professors are looking to the future of higher education.

 

In a recent article in The New York Times, President Emeritus of Harvard University, Lawrence H. Summers, recommends that universities embrace change. In the article, he suggests that curriculum be altered to reflect changes in technology. Summers believes that technology can improve post-secondary education if universities are open to transforming the classroom. 

 

Learning should focus on how to "process and use information" rather than on content memorization, Summers writes. With the breadth of knowledge available through the use of technology, students must learn how to use these tools to discover answers to problems. Traditional lecture halls should be transformed into "active learning classrooms," Summers suggests, asking students "to actively use the knowledge they are acquiring," rather than prove content mastery.

 

For example, electronic textbooks allow for information to be constantly revised and updated in real time. Electronic textbooks also provide information in a variety of formats, incorporating audio and visual content with reading material. "Think of a music text in which you can hear pieces of music as you read, or a history text in which you can see film clips about what you are reading," Summers adds. Interactive diagrams, graphs, and case studies engage students at a new level of learning. In addition, increased competition among textbook writers, creators, and publishers will drive up the quality of learning materials.

 

Technology permits collaboration among educators as well. Professors can use video lectures from top experts in any given field, and students can listen to the lectures at their own pace. This allows for classroom time to be focused on discussion rather than lecture.

 

Summers also stresses the importance of learning through teamwork exercises. According to Summers, more employers are evaluating individuals on how they interact with others and collaborate in groups, rather than on test scores or grades. Employers are increasingly looking for employees who can solve a problem with a team.

 

Summers' final suggestion for improving curriculum is to open the door of the classroom to the world. The expansion of technology has made our neighbors overseas seem closer. "The world is much more open and events abroad affect the lives of Americans more than ever before," Summers writes. Classroom discussions should include global examples and students should be encouraged to engage in international learning experiences.

Featured Tutor: Elisabeth Angeles

 

As a teenager, Elisabeth Angeles led a dog sled team through Alaska's wilderness after school. Now she tutors math students after school for A+ Test Prep and Tutoring. Elisabeth says she can relate to many of the frustrations that her students encounter while learning math.  

 

When she first took calculus, Elisabeth thought she disliked it. She figured the material was too difficult to fully understand. Rather than give up, Elisabeth applied a steady work ethic to learn each step, and then one day it clicked. "Once it made sense, I thought, hey, this is pretty cool," she said. Her most dreaded subject quickly transformed into her favorite. Just a few years later, she received a BA in Arts and Sciences with a minor in math at the University of Alaska, Fairbanks.

 

Now Elisabeth's favorite thing about tutoring is helping students discover that moment when everything clicks. At this point "learning becomes fun and interesting, not just a chore." It is "so much fun to be with students when they finally get it" and the confusion clears, she said.

 

Elisabeth, who has tutored for several years, and has worked as a school math specialist, recently relocated to Philadelphia. She began tutoring with A+ in September, 2011. She currently tutors eight students.

 

Many of Elisabeth's students face the challenge of grasping the principles of a subject. "In a classroom full of students, it is often difficult for a teacher to have the time to go back and review all of the basic concepts," said Elisabeth. When new material is introduced, a confused student can be stuck in a constant game of catch-up.


Elisabeth believes using real-world examples is the best way to help students see the problems in a different way. She prefers to teach with tangible objects to demonstrate a practical example, rather than relying on worksheets. "I don't want them just memorizing answers," she said. "I want them to understand what's going on."

 

Elisabeth's favorite advice to students is to "work on it a little bit every day." In order to learn and digest the material, students should devote 10 to 20 minutes each day to review, rather than a 2-hour cram session on the weekend before a test, she said.

 

Elisabeth cautions parents to watch how much they bash a subject. "Don't go around talking about how much you hated math. This just reinforces that math was horrible," she said. "It builds the apprehension in their minds." Elisabeth feels it is okay for parents to admit that a subject was difficult. However they should stress that it is possible to conquer the challenge.
