Featured Student: Rachel French

 

Rachel French, a junior at Academy of Notre Dame de Namur in Villanova, was anxious about the SAT because she didn't think she was a strong standardized test taker. Thankfully working with A+ helped changed that.

 

Over the course of four months, Diana Hong, Rachel's tutor, went to Rachel's house about once each week to provide a 1.5-hour SAT Preparation lesson. Rachel liked working with Diana because it was "personalized and wasn't something I dreaded. It was enjoyable," she said.

 

Rachel loved the fact that she could call Diana each week to tell her what day and time worked best for her schedule. As an avid athlete with Notre Dame's softball team in the spring, tennis in the fall, and recreational soccer throughout, Rachel has a tight schedule. Having a tutor come to Rachel's home was a much better fit for her scheduling needs than a group class that meets at the same time each week.

 

Some weeks if they were working hard on a lesson, Diana would stay a little longer to review additional questions. Rachel was assigned homework each week, including SAT practice tests, to prepare for the next week's lesson.

 

When Diana arrived at her house, they would work through the tests, going over each of the questions that Rachel struggled with.

 

"One of the reasons we worked so well together was that Rachel was committed to the process," Diana said. Rachel finished all of her homework, was excellent at receiving feedback on each of her practice tests, and "she came to the table motivated," Diana said.

 

The pair concentrated on techniques and strategies for completing the critical reading questions. They also worked to improve Rachel's writing skills, focusing on what the SAT essay readers look for.

 

When it came time for Rachel to take the actual SAT, she felt ready. "I wasn't that nervous. I'd taken 10 or 12 tests. I knew what to expect on the day of the test, so it kept the stress levels low," she said.

 

After taking the official test two times, Rachel placed in the 99th percentile according to The College Board. Her final scores improved 290 points with the help of A+.

 

Rachel has not yet decided where to apply for college, but hopes to attend medical school after college. In her free time, besides enjoying sports, Rachel plays the piano and spends the summers working at a restaurant in Ocean City, NJ.  

What to Do if Your Child Has Been Waitlisted

 

By Golda Steier, Owner of Precision Admission

 

Spring is in the air, the flowers are blooming, and we all know what that means (well at least those of us with high school seniors do) -- college decisions are arriving daily.  

 

What if your student has been waitlisted at the school of his or her dreams? Here are some tips for your teen:

 

  • Find out if the wait list is ranked and if so, what your position is.
  • Ask what percentage of students have been accepted from the wait list in recent years.
  • Ask about the college's policies regarding financial aid and housing for students admitted from the wait list. Will these services be available later on?
  • If you want to remain active on the wait list, return the reply postcard indicating your intention. (You should not be asked for a deposit at this time.)
  • Write a letter to the dean of admissions affirming your desire to attend. If it is your first choice, say so.
  • Include any significant new information in your letter, such as grades, awards, and activities. Also include anything else that addresses areas that might have been initially lacking in your application.
  • Ask your counselor to send a similar note conveying your enthusiasm and desire to attend.
  • Consider sending an additional recommendation from someone who knows you well.
  • Don't overdo it -- restate your strong interest and provide relevant data, but don't harass the admissions office.
  • Send your end-of-year grades if the wait list has not yet been closed.
  • Make sure you send a deposit to one of the colleges where you have been accepted to secure your spot.

 

Typically, students are not notified that they have been accepted from the wait list until after May 1. This is why you must send a deposit to another college or university. This also means that if you are accepted from the wait list, you will most likely lose the deposit you already made to the other school.

 

Please note that the rate of acceptance from a wait list is often relatively low. To see the wait list statistics for a particular school, visit College Board. Search for the school, and click on the tab labeled "Applying."

 

Remember, even if you are not admitted to your "dream" school, there are plenty of opportunities to have a great college experience!

SAT and ACT Test Takers Face Tighter Security

Last year 3,000 standardized tests were recalled for irregularities and more than 20 students were arrested in Long Island for cheating on the SAT or ACT. As a result, officials with both testing agencies are boosting security for college entrance exams.

 

The College Board and ACT Inc. have teamed up to create new security procedures for test takers. Beginning this fall, students registering for either the SAT or ACT will have to provide a photograph during the registration process. Students registering online will upload a current digital photo and those signing up for tests through the mail will enclose a photo with the registration form. In addition to submitting a photo, students will be required to report both their gender and the name of the high school they attend.  

  

"The image will be printed on each test taker's admissions ticket, which will then be checked against the photo IDs they present at testing centers," reported Eric Hoover in an article in The Chronicle of Higher Education. Students will be required to show the ticket with photo and an eligible ID when they enter the test, each time they leave and come back to the testing room, and when the tests are collected.

 

The photos will also be uploaded to a database administered by and accessible to high school, college, and university officials, along with the ETS Office of Testing Security.   

The photo ID requirement is an effort to combat impersonation, the main charge levied against more than 50 students suspected of wrongdoing in Nassau County, NY in 2011. Authorities discovered that dozens of students from multiple schools were paying other students to take the SAT and ACT in their name.  


Also, for the first time, SAT and ACT score reports will automatically be sent to each student's high school, along with the student's photo. In the past, students could choose not to have their scores sent to their high school. However the test agencies believe the new policy will provide another line of defense against impersonation.

 
Another new security measure is an agreement that students must sign prior to taking their exam. The agreement certifies identity, warning students that impersonation could lead to criminal prosecution.

 

In addition to tightened security to combat impersonations, students will no longer be able to:

 

  • Do "stand-by" testing on the day of the test (students must now register in advance)
  • Change their test location or test date on the day of the test
  • Change the test selected (SAT or SAT Subject Tests) on the day of the test

 

The security measures for both exams will go into effect at the start of the 2012-2013 academic year. For more detailed information about the new security measures, visit the policy pages on the SAT and ACT websites.
