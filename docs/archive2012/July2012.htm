Hopeful Students Perform Better

 

Dictionary.com defines hope as "the feeling that what is wanted can be had, or that events will turn out for the best." Researchers have made many attempts to understand how hope plays into success, and now they are finally finding some answers.

 

Allie Grasgreen examined this research in her Inside Higher Ed article, "Here's Hoping." In her compilation, Grasgreen found that "a growing (but still small) body of research is finding that students with high levels of hope get better grades and graduate at higher rates than those with lower levels, and that the presence of hope in a student is a better predictor of grades and class ranking than standardized test scores."

 

In one study at a Midwestern state college, graduation rates were 16 percent higher for hopeful students compared with less hopeful students. Hopeful Indiana law students had more success in school and higher LSAT scores than those with less hope. A study in Britain showed hope drives success more than personality, intelligence or prior academic success.

 

The real question Grasgreen contemplated was whether or not it's possible to enhance hope. According to her studies, some experts believe that hope can be learned. "Train a student to visualize his goals and see how he'll achieve them, even when obstacles arise, and hope will follow," she reported.

 

Universities across the country are testing the effectiveness of hope training. In California, researchers had Santa Clara University students participate in a 90-minute "intervention," where they worked on goal-mapping, changing mindsets to hopeful thinking, and visualization exercises. The study demonstrated that students who experienced the interventions were more likely to have made progress in goal achievement at the one-month follow-up than those who didn't.

 

The lead researcher on the study, counseling psychology professor David Feldman, said schools could boost hope levels by hosting periodic workshops, creating hope-related websites, and introducing hope to the classroom through instructor's language. It can be difficult to shed the messages of hopelessness in our world, with a troubled economy, and the prevalence of people losing jobs and homes, he said. Educators have a "unique opportunity to inspire" and "give some of that hope back," Feldman added.

 

At Chaffey College in California, the faculty is taking lessons in building and inspiring hope. Professors often misunderstand students' behavior, assuming a general disinterest when students aren't engaging in discussion. In reality students might simply be afraid of being wrong, according to Grasgreen's article. Educators need to learn to effectively offer advice, help, and encouragement, said Laura Hope, Chaffey's Dean of Instructional Support.

 

Grasgreen cited Robert Sternberg, a psychologist and vice president at Oklahoma State University, who said that educational institutions should focus on giving purpose during lessons, and providing meaning to studies, rather than just having students memorize facts. According to Sternberg, "Educators need to let students know that it's possible to overcome challenges in learning and that with hard work, success is achievable."

 

Turning Short-Term Memory
into Long-Term Memory

 

To better understand the role that illness, injury, and learning disabilities play in your child's ability to recall or memorize information, it's important to understand how the brain works.

 

The ability to convert short-term memory (memorizing information for just a few minutes) to long-term memory (information that is learned, stored, and can be retrieved later), involves a process in the brain called "consolidation," according to Alison Preston, an assistant professor for Austin's Center for Learning and Memory at the University of Texas.

 

Preston's article in Scientific American breaks down the consolidation process that occurs within the brain. When you take in new information, or learn something, your brain files the information in different areas, depending on the content. For example, if you learn that the oldest tree in your backyard is an oak tree, the visual element may be stored in one section of the brain, the auditory element in another, and the smell in another. All of these sensory elements must communicate with each other in order for you to put together the full memory.

 

A small S-shaped region of the brain called the hippocampus assists in connecting all of the pieces of the memory. Within minutes or hours of taking in new material, neurons in the brain begin to change structure and function. Connections between neurons begin to form or strengthen to allow communication between the cells. Eventually, in what may take days or years, your brain reorganizes the network of cells distributed in different compartments of the brain that process the memory, and it no longer needs the help of the hippocampus to communicate, Preston noted.

 

The first cellular and molecular changes are a form of consolidation--that of short-term memory. The "systems-level" consolidation, or conversion to long-term memory, is a much slower process, according to Preston.

 

Often when people have difficulty with short-term memory, the hippocampus has been damaged through injury or illness, such as Alzheimer's disease, Preston added. The hippocampus is unable to connect all of the bits of information stored in different compartments of the brain, before it is fully consolidated. "Interestingly, such a disruption does not impair memory for facts or events that have already been consolidated," she said. "Thus, an amnesiac with hippocampal damage would not be able to learn the names of our current presidential candidates but would be able to recall the identity of our 16th president."

 

There are ways to "train your brain" to move information into long-term memory, according to Colleen Bain, author of Overcomers Inc. Bain works with children who suffer from attention deficit disorder. She believes that by including information in an enjoyable emotional experience, you can help a child's brain create new cognitive pathways.

 

Bain uses the example of how a child can remember every detail of a favorite family trip that occurred several years in the past. If you can provide that kind of emotional experience while learning, a child can speed the consolidation process, Bain said.

 

Research has also demonstrated that the brain uses sleep to amplify the consolidation process, according to Preston's article, giving validity to the well-known phrase "sleep on it." In addition, sleep deprivation can disturb hippocampus activity, making it difficult to retain new memories, Preston concluded. This research provides yet another reason to get a good night's sleep!

Featured Student:
Gabby Tofig

 

High School: Council Rock North

 

SAT Score Improvement: 330 Points

 

Three-season varsity athlete and distinguished honors student Gabby Tofig knows the value of hard work. She also knows that focus is the key to success.

 

After taking the SAT twice and not reaching her personal goal, Gabby wasn't about to give up. The "crash course" SAT prep class that she took in school just didn't give her the boost she needed to achieve her potential. "My SAT didn't correlate in numbers with my grades," Gabby said.

 

Following the advice of a friend's mom, Gabby signed up for one-to-one test prep tutoring with A+. She was matched with Julie Mueller, who scheduled eleven 1.5-hour sessions with Gabby before she would face the SAT for the third time.

 

With Gabby's end-goal mindset, the pair based all lessons on achieving a certain score. Julie helped Gabby get a better feel for the SAT language so she would understand what questions were truly asking. "Sometimes questions are awkwardly worded," Gabby said, "which confused me during the test." Julie taught Gabby the logic behind the questions, and assisted her with tricks for deciphering how to solve them. "Even when I didn't do well, Julie helped me figure out what I wasn't doing," Gabby said.  

 

"Gabby was a wonderful student to work with because she was tenacious," Julie said. At first Gabby's practice tests weren't reflecting the hard work she was putting into her test prep. "She didn't give up though; she kept working at the concepts from different angles, trying to make sure she understood the underlying ideas, the framework of the test, and the way the questions worked so that she could earn the score she wanted," Julie added.

 

Gabby was nervous at the start of her SAT. She had a big track meet right after the test and her performance at the meet would determine whether or not she would qualify for districts. Before worrying about her meet, she needed to focus on the test.

 

"Five hours is really tough," Gabby said. "The hardest part is staying focused, and I managed to do that." Keeping her mind on the task at hand, Gabby carefully worked through one problem at a time.

 

Gabby's goal was to get a combined score of 1200 on the math and reading sections. With a little prep from A+, Gabby surpassed her expectations, scoring a combined 1300 on math and reading, and beating her previous SAT score by 330 points. She earned a 620 on the reading portion, a 680 on the math section and 670 on writing. "I was extremely excited, my entire family was excited," Gabby said. "Julie gave me the confidence I needed on the test."

 

Gabby just completed her junior year at Council Rock North High School in Newtown, PA. She plays varsity field hockey and runs on the varsity team for both indoor and outdoor track. She is currently deciding between signing with Lehigh or Temple to play field hockey in college. In addition to her accomplishments as an athlete, Gabby is a member of National Honor Society, volunteers regularly with her school's community service club, and works as a lifeguard during the summer.
