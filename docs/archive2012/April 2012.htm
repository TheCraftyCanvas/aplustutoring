A Look at the SAT Subject Tests

 

As the end of the school year nears and courses begin to wrap up, high school students should consider registering for the SAT Subject Tests this June. These tests provide supplemental evaluation of school subjects to accompany SAT or ACT scores on college applications.

 

There are 20 SAT Subject Tests. Each test focuses on curriculum from one of five areas: math, science, English, history, and foreign languages. Click here for a full list of Subject Tests offered by The College Board.

 

The SAT Subject Tests have gained popularity over the last 5 years, as more colleges require or recommend additional test scores. According to The College Board, more than 500,000 students take the tests each year.

 

The University of Pennsylvania, University of Delaware, Lehigh University, Lafayette College, St. Joseph's University, and Princeton University are among 60 institutions that require the Subject Tests with applications. A full list of these universities can be found on The College Board's website.

 

In order to determine whether it is necessary to take a Subject Test, students should research the requirements for the colleges to which they plan to apply. In some cases, colleges require that specific SAT Subject Test scores be submitted with an application for admission to certain majors or departments. 

 

Typically institutions use the tests to further evaluate the candidate or to help place students in proper courses. Even if Subject Test scores are not required or recommended on your student's application, taking the tests may be a good idea. The scores can showcase a student's mastery of a subject or interest in a particular area. SAT Subject Test scores may also help differentiate a student from others who are applying by demonstrating the student's willingness to go "above and beyond" in the application process.

 

Prior to taking Subject Tests students should complete the highest level of high school coursework available in each subject area. This makes June the ideal time for students to take the SAT Subject Tests because the course material is fresh their in minds. A+ Test Prep and Tutoring provides one-to-one tutoring for the SAT Subject Tests, either in one of our two offices, or in your home. Students typically receive four 1.5-hour tutoring sessions per Subject Test.

Students are generally encouraged to take SAT Subject Tests in their strongest subject areas. The College Board offers an Official Study Guide that includes one full-length practice test in each subject. Consult the College Board's  list of average SAT Subject Test scores to see how your student's score on the practice test compares to others who have taken the test.  

 

Scores on the SAT Subject Tests use the same score range as the SAT, with 800 being the highest possible score, and 200 the lowest. Students are allowed to take the exams more than once and can request that The College Board report only the highest score.

 

The Subject Tests are offered six times a year on the same dates that the SAT is offered: January, May, June, October, November, and December. Note that the SAT Subject Tests are NOT offered on the March testing date. Students can take up to three Subject Tests on one test date, but can't take the SAT on the same day. To register for the SAT Subject Tests, visit The College Board's website to sign up online or via mail.

Featured Student: Heather Zezzo

 

Central Bucks West High School Junior Heather Zezzo is not only a talented field hockey player,  but she is also a science fair participant, a Chinese speaker, and a member of the Junior National Dragon Boat team. Despite her hard work in school and a very good GPA, Heather's SAT scores were not as high as she wanted them to be. She knew she could do better.  

 

After trying a small group class to study for the SAT her sophomore year, Heather realized she wanted to find something more personal. "Everyone was on a different level, and you didn't want to ask questions because it would hold everyone else back," she said.

 

Heather decided to contact A+ Test Prep after her mother heard good things about A+ from a fellow field hockey mom. The one-to-one format seemed like a perfect match.

 

Heather was paired up with A+ tutor Julie Mueller. Since Heather's calendar is packed, Julie worked out a flexible schedule, going to her house between activities.

 

Heather took the SATs in January and March.  "When I got to the SAT test I was well prepared with all of my math formulas and various quotes from authors" Heather said. "I arrived a little early for the test so I had time to write anything that I wanted. I wrote about being nervous, what I was going to do after testing finished, how I felt, anything just to get the blood flowing to my hand and to get my brain starting to think. It really helped me to start getting into that mode of 'okay testing now' so that when the 25-minute essay period began, I wasn't still waking up."

 

On her SATs in March, Heather and her mom were "elated and surprised" to see that she scored 2050, a 150-point increase from the SAT she took in October.

 

"I was a wreck the night before my scores came in," Heather said, even though she had a good feeling leaving the test. "Clicking and seeing that I reached 700 in two categories -- I actually jumped up and down I was so excited! I was so proud of myself and so happy that the hard work that my tutor Julie and I put in really paid off." 

 

"Heather exemplifies the kind of student that makes the best use of this program," Julie said. "She didn't want to just learn the material; she wanted to understand it and use it." Julie was also impressed by how well rounded Heather was in all of her activities. "She has a lot going for her," Julie said. "She's not just bright, but she's excelled in a lot of different things."

 

Since her tutoring experience, Heather has been keeping herself busy touring colleges and attending field hockey team clinics at a number of Ivy League colleges. Next year she will participate in the Junior Dragon Boat World Cup in Budapest.

Technology Corner: TED-Ed
TED-Ed

 

TED, the nonprofit organization famous for broadcasting inspiring lectures from leaders, innovators, and thinkers around the world, is launching a lesson-sharing website for educators this month.

 

TED Conferences LLC is dedicated to "ideas worth spreading." Its products aim to publicize big ideas by bringing together technology, design, and entertainment to amplify the voices of the movers and shakers of this world.

 

Now TED is breaking into the education arena with TED-Ed. The idea behind the new offering is to "pair extraordinary educators with talented animators to produce a new library of curiosity-igniting videos." The tagline for TED-ED is "lessons worth sharing." TED is asking the best educators to come forth, suggest lesson ideas, and send in recorded lectures to create videos to share with other teachers. The organization then animates the recorded lessons to illustrate the ideas and concepts the teachers present.

 

The animation with teacher voiceover is edited to create a 5-to 12-minute video that teachers around the world can use free of charge to open classroom discussions and to transition into new topics.

           

TED's goal is to spark interest in school lessons and "to capture and to amplify the voice of the world's greatest teachers," according to the organization's website.

           

Lesson plans featured on the new website include "The Secret Life of Plankton," "The Real Origin of the Franchise," and "The Power of Simple Words."

           

According to an article by Jeffrey R. Young on The Chronicle of Higher Education's Wired Campus blog, most of TED-Ed's first videos will be created for a high school audience. Young suggests the video service "is unlikely to revolutionize classroom education, but it could provide a digital upgrade to the old idea of classroom filmstrips."

           

To learn more about TED-Ed, watch this video or visit the organization's website.
